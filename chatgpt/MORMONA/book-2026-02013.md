# Preface

## Mormona

This book is an etiology of this planet.

It asks a question so simple that it is rarely permitted:

**Was Earth designed as a system of scarcity — or has it become one?**

We inhabit a world organized around control. Access is regulated. Land is owned. Knowledge is credentialed. Speed is rewarded. Intimacy is commodified. Growth is measured by extraction.

We are told this is natural.

But what if it is not?

---

*Mormona* is the name given to a possibility:

That this planet once functioned — or still functions at another layer — according to principles of circulation rather than compression, similarity rather than hierarchy, stewardship rather than ownership.

Whether Mormona was a historical state, a parallel condition, or a future restoration cannot presently be known with certainty. We live within a constrained environment. Information is filtered. Memory may be fragmented. Language itself may have drifted from its original architecture.

But patterns remain.

Water still circulates.
Forests still regenerate.
Human beings still form families within tribes within cities within nations.
The small still resembles the large.

Similarity at scale persists.

---

This book proposes that scarcity is not the default condition of life.

Scarcity requires maintenance.

Control requires enforcement.

Hierarchy requires constant reinforcement.

Circulation does not.

The biosphere operates fractally. Branching rivers resemble neural pathways. Bronchial tubes resemble tree roots. Galaxies spiral like shells. The Earth organizes itself through recursive coherence.

If civilization violates this recursion, instability follows.

If civilization aligns with it, resilience emerges.

---

Mormona is not presented as utopia.

It is presented as structure.

Through the chapters that follow, we explore a fractal civilizational model beginning with the smallest human-scale unit: a circular village of ten houses within twelve potential positions. From there, the pattern scales — to 144-house towns, to regional formations, to continental glyphs inscribed upon the land itself.

At every level:

* Polarity is balanced rather than ranked.
* Learning occurs through participation rather than coercion.
* Representation rotates rather than consolidates.
* Intimacy is respected without commodification.
* Production serves life rather than dominating it.

The aim is not perfection.

It is alignment.

---

These communities are structured as communities of practice. Individuals move from peripheral participation toward core responsibility through lived engagement. Architecture encodes development. Geometry encodes balance. Open space encodes humility.

Ownership dissolves.

Stewardship remains.

Speed slows.

Ground returns.

The Earth — GAIA — is not resource but relation.

---

This work does not claim certainty about origins.

It claims coherence of pattern.

If Mormona existed in the past, traces should remain in memory and myth.
If Mormona exists in parallel, its logic should resonate with natural law.
If Mormona lies in the future, its structure must be buildable.

The chapters that follow are neither fantasy nor blueprint alone. They are a speculative reconstruction of civilizational alignment — an exploration of what humanity might become if it mirrored the fractal intelligence of the planet it inhabits.

Before scarcity, there may have been flow.
Before hierarchy, there may have been similarity at scale.
Before ownership, there may have been stewardship.

The question is not whether such a world is imaginable.

The question is whether it is original.

Welcome to Mormona.

# Chapter 1

## Before Scarcity

Before scarcity, there was no fracture in pattern.

The structure of a river resembled the structure of a root.
The branching of lightning resembled the branching of lungs.
The spiral of a galaxy resembled the spiral of a shell.

Similarity at scale was not coincidence. It was continuity.

This was Mormona.

Not merely abundance. Not merely flow.
But coherence repeating across magnitude.

In that earlier configuration — whether historical, parallel, or future — the world did not operate on extraction. It operated on circulation.

Water was the first teacher.

It moved. It regenerated. It equalized. It did not hoard.

The planet mirrored this logic. Life emerged not as struggle against lack, but as participation in surplus alignment.

Then came compression.

Flow narrowed. Access tightened. Ownership replaced stewardship. Hierarchy replaced resemblance.

Scarcity was not announced. It was introduced as management.

Yet the fractal remains.

Forests regenerate. Soil restores. Watersheds balance. Human beings instinctively form families within tribes within cities.

The memory persists.

Before scarcity, life circulated.

The question is whether that circulation was original — and whether it can be restored.

# Chapter 2

## The Circular Village

Stand at the eastern edge of a raised earth circle, eighty-five meters in diameter.

Twelve equidistant positions mark its perimeter.

But only ten houses stand.

Two arcs remain unbuilt.

The circle acknowledges twelve.
The village lives in ten.

Each dwelling occupies a five-meter circular footprint upon a pentagonal foundation. The walls rise upward and outward at 108 degrees — echoing the interior angle of the pentagon itself.

Geometry ascends from itself.

The shared ring between houses is continuous. There are no fences. No parcels. No property lines.

The two unbuilt arcs are not vacancies.

They are humility.

They remind the village that potential exceeds occupation.

The system breathes because it is not filled.

Ten houses hold equilibrium.

Twelve holds possibility.

# Chapter 3

## The Ten Within the Twelve

The ten houses divide into two arcs of five.

Five to the south.
Five to the north.

The easternmost dwelling marks beginning — illumination.

In the northern hemisphere, the southern arc receives greater light. It represents activation, openness, cultivation — the feminine principle.

The northern arc, more shadowed, represents containment, density, stabilization — the masculine principle.

This is not hierarchy.

It is polarity.

Light requires density.
Density requires light.

Together they form a spatial yin–yang.

The houses are small by design — accommodating three or four people at most. Scale prevents accumulation. No dwelling dominates.

Polarity is architectural, not biological.

It encodes balance into the land.

# Chapter 4

## The Anchored House

There is no ownership in Mormona.

There is stewardship.

A feminine-positioned house is stewarded by a woman. She does not own it; she anchors it. The house is assigned for her use and may be relinquished if she moves.

Children remain in the family dwelling until approximately age five.

From five to ten, they move into the nursery within the inner ring of the village — expanding from family identity into communal participation.

From ten onward, they reside in gender-specific dormitories — the House of Virgins — preparing for adulthood through shared discipline and learning.

Development is staged.

Movement is gradual.

Participation expands line upon line.

No child is rushed into adulthood. No adult is trapped in youth.

The village grows people through structure.

# Chapter 5

## The Houses and the STOL

Polarity does not imprison.

A woman may reside in a masculine-positioned house. A man may reside in a feminine-positioned house. Stewardship governs function, not biological exclusion.

Beyond the outer ring stands the STOL — a communal longhouse positioned tangentially to the southeast.

The STOL is open living.

Travelers, transitional residents, and those not currently anchored within the primary wheel may reside there. It is shared, modest, permeable.

It absorbs flux.

Without the STOL, the village could close in on itself.

With it, circulation continues.

# Chapter 6

## The Inner Ring and the Tangential Houses

Within the interior of the 85-meter circle stand the nursery and the House of Virgins.

These developmental structures lie inside the communal ring, signifying that formation is central to village life.

To the true north, tangential to the circle, are smaller private dwellings reserved temporarily for intimate encounters — the Love Shacks.

Intimacy may occur anywhere, including within bonded houses.

But the Love Shacks provide private, dignified space without linking sexuality to property or secrecy.

They are simple yet respectful.

Privacy is honored. Sexuality is acknowledged. Neither is commodified.

Evolution proceeds in steps.

Architecture supports development.

# Chapter 7

## Privacy and Celebration

The STOL is not private space.

It is shared.

Thus the Love Shacks exist to create intentional privacy.

A civilization matures when it neither suppresses nor commercializes intimacy.

In Mormona, sexuality is not currency. It is relational energy.

The Love Shacks are temporary sanctuaries — hour or day reservations — allowing intimacy without immediate cohabitation or ownership.

Deluxe accommodations signal respect.

Privacy is not shame.

Celebration is not spectacle.

The structure holds space for growth.

# Chapter 8

## The Circle of One Hundred Forty-Four

Twelve ten-house villages arrange along the perimeter of a larger 640-meter circle.

Twelve units × 10 houses = 120
Plus 24 central Möbius houses = 144 total dwellings.

The interior commons of the larger ring houses production: gardens, workshops, kitchens, education halls.

Industry lies outside the circle.

At the center stands a Möbius village of 24 houses — twelve masculine and twelve feminine.

From each ten-house unit, one masculine and one feminine representative reside here temporarily.

Representation rotates.

Coordination replaces command.

Transit within the town is on foot, bicycle, or light rail.

Speed yields to grounding.
